## The Problem I Lived Every Day

When I joined TKIET Warananagar for my B.Tech, I quickly realized something: **there was no food delivery here**.

Swiggy? Not available. Zomato? Nope. Every app I opened, the message was the same — *"We don't deliver to your area."*

I was a hostel student. I had no vehicle, limited access to restaurants, and on long study nights, the only option was to either starve or walk 20 minutes to the nearest dhaba that might be closing soon.

I knew this wasn't just my problem. Thousands of students across India face this exact situation — in college towns, small cities, and rural areas where the big delivery giants simply don't operate.

## The Idea

So I thought: **what if I built it myself?**

Not just a side project. A real product. A real business.

That's when FeedoZone was born — a hyperlocal food delivery platform specifically designed for underserved markets.

## Building Alone

I had zero team. Zero funding. Just a laptop, a lot of chai, and a determination to ship.

I started with market research — talking to students, vendors, and restaurant owners. I validated the idea. People wanted it. Vendors wanted it.

Then I built it:

- **React Native** for the mobile app
- **Next.js** for the web platform
- **Firebase** for the backend and real-time data
- **Node.js + Express** for APIs

As a solo founder, I wore every hat:
- Product Manager
- Full Stack Developer
- UI/UX Designer
- Operations Lead
- Marketing
- Customer Support
- Vendor Onboarding

It was exhausting. And incredibly rewarding.

## What I Learned

Building a startup alone teaches you things no college class ever could:

1. **Ship fast, iterate faster** — perfect is the enemy of shipped
2. **Talk to users constantly** — your assumptions will be wrong
3. **Operations matter as much as code** — a broken delivery promise kills trust faster than a broken UI
4. **Resilience is a skill** — things will break, users will complain, keep going

## Where We Are Now

FeedoZone is live. We've onboarded vendors. We're processing orders.

It's small. But it's real. And it's growing.

The journey from "I have an idea" to "I have a live product" in a few months — solo — is something I'm proud of.

This is just the beginning.
